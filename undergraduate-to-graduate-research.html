Graduate research

From undergraduate study to graduate research

A reflection after joining the student panel at the 2nd Bellini College REU Symposium.

May 1, 2026 · PhD workflow · Research experience

On Friday, May 1, 2026, I had the opportunity to be one of the PhD students on a panel about transitioning from undergraduate studies to graduate school during the 2nd Bellini College REU Symposium. It was a great experience because helping the next generation of students is part of life at the university. The panel also motivated me to write my first post here, where I want to discuss a little of my experience as an undergraduate student, becoming an undergraduate research assistant, and then moving into the PhD role.

How I found research

Let’s talk briefly about my timeline. As an undergraduate student, I was taking courses, as expected, and during my junior year I took Control of Mobile Robots. That course sparked my interest in robotics, so I started taking more courses on the topic. The next one was Introduction to Robotics with Dr. Sun, who is now my advisor.

At the time, I did not think I was going to start working in research, especially not in Dr. Sun’s lab, RPAL. But I went to an event where faculty presented their research, and Dr. Sun was one of the faculty members there. I immediately became interested in the grasping and manipulation work he was showing, so I sent an email asking about research opportunities with RPAL. We scheduled a meeting, the meeting went well, and I started working in the laboratory during my senior year.

The panel

With that out of the way, let’s talk about the panel.

At the table there were two PhD students and two undergraduate students involved in research. The questions were shy at the start, but they started coming, getting better, and by the end we were all talking a lot for each answer. Overall, it was a great experience. Here are some of the questions we talked about.

Work, health, and routine

One of the faculty responsible for undergraduate engagement asked about how to manage work and fun. My take on this has changed during my PhD. I started with my focus glued to the educational and professional parts of my life, which is manageable for a while. Sometimes you do need to work long hours, spend some weekends working, and miss some social gatherings.

But life is not only about managing. It is about living.

After I finished my classes, I started working out more, eating healthier, and taking more intentional breaks. I was not giving up on my health to work more. And, in a way, taking care of my health made my work better. With a more structured routine, I can work longer hours when needed, wake up earlier, and have much better throughput. But the important part is that the routine became more sustainable.

Questions with answers, questions with direction

A student also asked about the biggest struggle, and for this one I had an easy answer. As an international student, moving away from family and friends is hard. Trying to rebuild a support system while starting a PhD is challenging and requires a lot of patience.

Also, one of the biggest jumps when transitioning from class work, either undergraduate or graduate, to research work is that the questions you work on in class usually have answers. The questions you work on in research usually have a direction. And most of the time, you do not know which direction to go at the start of the process.

Finding a research question

The last question I want to talk about here came from another undergraduate student. He asked how finding a research question works. Is there a structured plan to follow, or is it always an insight that comes from somewhere?

The start of my answer got some laughs:

If you, or anyone here, knows the plan, please tell me.

Because I really believe the answer is a bit of both. As a PhD student, reading papers will be part of your weeks, and perhaps part of your days. With time, you get a better perspective on what questions are being asked in your field and, perhaps more importantly, what answers are being published.

But, as computer scientists know from reinforcement learning, we need to balance exploitation and exploration. Reading only papers from your own field might make you miss interesting work that could be applied or adapted to your problem. Sometimes, reading a paper from a different field is what brings that Eureka moment.

Closing thoughts

To close this first post, I think pursuing a PhD is a hard decision, and students should think carefully before taking that leap. But it can also be a rewarding experience, one that teaches you a lot about your work, your field, and yourself in the process.

If you are an undergraduate student thinking about research, you do not need to know the full plan from the beginning. Start by getting close to questions that interest you. Go to faculty research talks. Talk to graduate students. Send the email. Try a small project. Read a little. And pay attention to the questions that keep pulling your curiosity back.
